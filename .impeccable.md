## Design Context

### Users
Mixed audience of Indonesian youth (18-30) and global young professionals — browsed casually on both mobile and desktop. Users are early-career or pre-career, seeking jobs, courses, and programs that can genuinely change their trajectory. They are aspirational but not elite — they want to feel like they belong in professional spaces, not intimidated by them.

### Brand Personality
**Energetic, bold, youthful.** Rise Social is a career launchpad with momentum — not a polished corporate tool, not a playful toy. Think of it like a premium sneaker brand applied to career development: clean and refined in execution, but unmistakably alive. The brand should feel like a mentor who's also young, sharp, and genuinely excited about what's possible.

Brand colors reinforce this:
- **Rise Orange** (`#fe5b11`, oklch ~65% C:0.22 H:35) — the primary accent, used sparingly and intentionally for energy and emphasis
- **Deep Teal** (`#1bb1a0`, oklch ~62% C:0.13 H:185) — secondary, used for trust and calm contrast

### Aesthetic Direction
**Clean & refined with a bold typographic voice.** Generous white space, disciplined grid, no decoration for its own sake — but typography and color do the heavy lifting. The interface should feel effortless to use and visually mature. Light mode. No dark surfaces, no neon glows.

The memorable thing about Rise Social: **the orange means something.** It's not scattered everywhere — it appears at moments of importance: CTAs, active states, progress indicators. When it shows up, you notice.

Typography should be distinctive — not system defaults, not the usual edtech san-serifs. Look for a display/heading font that feels confident and human, paired with a clean text font with good legibility at small sizes.

### Design Principles
1. **Restraint earns attention.** Use the brand orange like punctuation: rarely, deliberately, with meaning. Overuse kills its power.
2. **Space is structure.** Generous padding and clear hierarchy make the interface feel trustworthy, not cramped. Young users shouldn't feel overwhelmed.
3. **Typography carries the personality.** Visual interest comes from type choices and weight contrast, not decorative shapes or busy backgrounds.
4. **Mobile-first, then expand.** Much of the audience is on phones. Layout decisions start from the smallest viewport.
5. **Confidence without exclusion.** The design should feel aspirational — like the user is leveling up — but never intimidating or gatekeeping.

### Anti-References
- Generic LinkedIn: cold, corporate, interchangeable job platform
- Dark mode SaaS aesthetic: cyan/purple glow on dark — completely wrong emotional register
- Overly playful: bubbly, cartoon-like, loses professional credibility
- Typical edtech (Coursera/Udemy): template-heavy, stock photos, rating stars everywhere
